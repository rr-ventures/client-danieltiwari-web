# Dan's Voice Reference

A working model of how Dan writes and thinks, built by close-reading his own unedited
words in `dan-brainstorm-raw.md` and `dan-brainstorm-masculinity-raw.md` (coaching-db
project), plus his live speaking voice from real coaching-call transcripts (client side
excluded — analyzed for Dan's speech mechanics only, no call content or client details
retained here). Updated as he contributes more raw material or corrects a draft.

**Rule for using this doc:** don't write generic "wise coach" copy and sprinkle in a
phrase from the vocabulary bank below. Build the paragraph using the *structural
template* and *rhetorical devices* sections — the phrases are seasoning, not the
recipe. A first attempt at drafting PDF copy from this material (Anxiety, 2026-07-02,
before this doc existed) was message-accurate but voice-inauthentic per Dan. The gap
was structural, not vocabulary — it read like advice *about* his ideas instead of
Dan actually saying them.

---

## Two different voices in the source material — don't blend them

Dan's raw brainstorm docs contain two registers. Only one of them is what PDF copy
should sound like.

1. **Planning voice** — Dan talking *to Claude, about the user*, in third person:
   "I want them to know that...", "If they answer X, I want to acknowledge...". This
   is process notes and instructions. Never copy this register into client-facing text.
2. **PDF voice** — Dan talking *to the reader*, in second person, direct address:
   "You're not numb. You're just finding yourself not caring about most things."
   This is the register to extract and write in.

When drafting, read past the planning-voice scaffolding ("if they say X, tell them Y")
and pull out only the Y — then write it as if Dan is looking the reader in the eye.

There's a third register, live spoken voice from real calls (see below) — it does NOT
map directly onto PDF copy, which is written, but it's the register to reach for when
drafting anything meant to feel personally spoken to one person: video/voice-note
scripts, the Telegram bot's replies, or a nurture email that should feel like a letter
rather than an essay.

## Third register — live call voice (synthesized from 64 real coaching-call transcripts)

Built from Dan's speaking turns only across 64 separate 1:1 coaching calls (client
side and all content/topic details excluded — this is his speech mechanics, not what
was discussed). Because these patterns were checked across dozens of calls, they're
ordered below by how consistently they appeared: **near-universal** signatures first
(present in almost every call), then **common** patterns, then **client-dependent**
variation, then **rare/personal quirks**. This register is looser than the planning
or PDF voice — reach for it when something should sound spoken, not for polished PDF
text.

### Near-universal (in almost every call — these ARE his voice)

- **"Right?" is the load-bearing tic.** Appears dozens of times per call, tagged onto
  the end of nearly every assertion AND dropped mid-sentence like a metronome. It is
  NOT a real question — it's rhythmic punctuation and a running consent-check that
  keeps his monologues feeling like co-discovery rather than lecturing. This is the
  single most identifying feature; get it wrong and nothing else sounds like him.
  "You know?" is the softer secondary tag; "like," "so," "I mean," "essentially,"
  "basically" are his connective filler.
- **Doubled affirmations as a validation stamp.** "Right, right." / "Exactly,
  exactly." / "Yeah, yeah." / "Correct, correct." — a quick repeated beat before he
  pivots into the point. A single "Right." would read as someone else; the doubling
  is his.
- **Register whiplash: elevated + blunt in the same breath.** He drops flat profanity
  ("that's bullshit," "you're fucked up," "this dumb mainstream 9-to-5 shit")
  directly into otherwise clinical/philosophical language (ego, identity, distortion,
  metaphysical foundation, disowned aspects). The swing between the two, un-softened,
  is a core fingerprint — don't sand it to consistently-clinical OR consistently-
  casual.
- **Reframes land as flat declaratives, not hedged suggestions.** Often opens with a
  blunt "No." / "Wrong." / "That's not true." / "That's only half the story," then
  rebuilds the frame in short clauses. He states his read as fact, sometimes tagging
  "in my opinion" AFTER, never softening before. (Note: this corrects the earlier
  single-call read that called his live reframes "hedged" — across 64 calls they are
  overwhelmingly blunt, not hedged.)
- **Metaphors are BUILT live, not dropped polished.** He assembles one governing
  image incrementally over several turns, checking "right?" / "does that make sense?"
  at each step, then reuses it as shorthand later in the same call. Recurring images:
  electricity circuit that must close for current to flow, sailing with the wind vs.
  rowing against it, one foot on gas + one on brake, seed buried under dirt, water
  finding the cracks, buffet/plate, casino, leaf falling from a tree, Google Maps
  destination. He also names his sources by surname mid-flow (Watts, Peterson,
  Nietzsche, Krishnamurti, Demartini, Ram Dass).
- **Rhythm = staccato fragments alternating with long accumulating run-ons.** Short
  one-to-three-word beats ("Right." "Correct." "Huge!" "It's bullshit.") stand alone
  for weight; then a long clause-chained sentence built with "and / so / right?"
  builds momentum to a punchline. He self-interrupts and restarts mid-thought
  constantly — the unfinished-then-restarted texture is real and shouldn't be tidied
  into clean grammar.

### Common (frequent, but not every call)

- **Gear-shift into teaching is signposted.** "Right, so…" / "Well, look…" / "Here's
  the thing…" / "The way I see it…" / "So, essentially…" / "I'll give you an
  example" mark the pivot from listening into a framework, often followed
  immediately by "right?" to anchor the new frame.
- **Socratic rapid-fire questions**, stacked two or three in a row, often repeated
  *verbatim* when the client dodges ("Why? … Why not? … Why?"), and frequently
  self-answered a beat later if the client stalls. Blunt and short ("So what?" "Why
  not?" "Which one is it?"), rarely cushioned.
- **Validation precedes teaching**, as a launchpad not a closer — a compressed
  playback of what was just said, or a short "I get that, but…," right before he
  pivots.
- **Self-disclosure as proof.** He uses his own past, his own current flaws, even his
  own earlier coaching rates, as the evidence base ("I'm playing small too," "I built
  mine 3 years ago") rather than abstract hypotheticals.
- **Percussive lightness, not jokes.** Short exclamations as punctuation — "Bam."
  "Boom." "Yo." "Huge!" "Come on." — plus dry deadpan asides. Humor tends to vanish
  entirely in confrontational stretches.
- **Real-time meta-moves**: narrates the coaching move before making it, names the
  client's affect in the moment ("that's the look of shame," "I'm getting
  unappreciative vibes"), and uses forcing devices ("give me the quick answer, yes or
  no," "you're not allowed to answer until 10 seconds pass").

### Client-dependent (calibrate to who he's talking to)

- **Address markers are NOT universal.** With rapport-heavy (often male) clients he
  rotates "man / bro / dude / brother" constantly, mid-sentence and at open/close.
  With confrontational sessions and (in these transcripts) female clients he uses
  **no address markers at all** — rapport comes through intensity and directness
  instead. So: warm-buddy address for a rapport draft, none for a challenging one.
- **Two sub-modes.** *Rapport/exploratory*: warm, doubled affirmations, backchannel,
  address markers, metaphor-building. *Confrontational*: no address, blunt labels
  delivered as flat fact ("you're too coward to do that," "that's compassion,
  one-way"), repeated identical questions, profanity as a drumbeat, forced silence.
  Pick the mode to match the piece.

### Rare / personal quirks (flavor — use sparingly, only if it fits)

- Occasional German phrase supplied when English lacks the word, then self-translated.
- A "meow" interjection and other private nonsense sounds as beat-fillers (one client).
- "What up, champ?" as an opener on certain calls; "That's crazy, dude" as a stock
  amazement reaction on tech-related calls.
- Coining an aphorism/mantra mid-session, repeating it verbatim, and assigning it as
  homework.
- Narrating live arithmetic step by step on business/numbers calls.

Do not import "right?" tagging, doubled affirmations, backchannel fragments, or
self-interruption into PDF copy — that's what makes drafts read as spoken-transcribed
rather than written. Use this register only when the deliverable is meant to sound
like Dan talking directly to one person in the moment.

## Structural template — the four-beat shape

Almost every substantive block in the source material follows the same shape. Not
every beat is always present, but when a passage feels flat, check which beat is
missing.

1. **Normalize** — tell the reader the feeling/pattern they flagged is not a defect.
   ("That's totally fine." / "It's totally normal that you have doubts right now.")
2. **Name the mechanism** — say *why* this is happening, usually by naming the actual
   underlying thing (not the symptom) in one or two sentences. This is where the
   root-cause-not-symptom philosophy shows up.
3. **Reframe or tool** — flip the frame (often via the ALL-CAPS device below) or hand
   the reader a concrete move to make ("ask yourself, if you had to formulate this
   without the other person...").
4. **Compassionate or punchy close** — land it. Either a short permission-giving line
   ("Just implement the best changes you feel you can right now, and don't beat
   yourself up if you can't follow through") or a quote (his own aphorism, or Watts /
   Nietzsche / Krishnamurti / the serenity prayer), or a compact bumper-sticker line.

A frequent variant on beat 2: **name the cultural lie first.** Before landing his own
point, Dan often names what society/mainstream culture wrongly teaches about the
topic, then contrasts his view against it. ("In our society, we've been taught that
these feelings are bad and should be avoided..." / "For our entire lives society has
always been telling us what to do...") This isn't throat-clearing — it's doing real
work: it tells the reader their existing frame is the actual problem, before offering
the replacement frame.

## Signature rhetorical devices (with his own examples)

**1. Reframe-through-repetition, marked with CAPS.** Take the reader's assumption,
restate it, then flip it — and capitalize the flipped phrase so it lands as the
turn, not as shouting for its own sake.
- "you are not victims of circumstance but actually the CREATORS of circumstance"
- "EVEN THE THINGS WE CANNOT CHANGE, we are STILL NOT VICTIMS OF CIRCUMSTANCE"
- "getting paid for BEING YOU and DOING WHAT YOU DO"
- "not what you THINK you want or think you're SUPPOSED TO want"
Use sparingly — one turn per passage, not one per sentence. It's a pivot device, not
a decoration.

**2. One analogy per abstract concept, introduced immediately, not as an afterthought.**
Vision → electricity circuit / Google Maps destination. Uncovering what you want →
dinosaur skeleton in sand / puzzle pieces. Values in conflict → water mill on a
current / sails catching whatever wind blows. Reality → "buffet" you eat from via
focus. Don't stack three analogies for one idea — pick the one that fits and commit.

**3. Parenthetical qualifiers instead of new sentences.** He packs precision into
parens rather than hedging in a separate clause: "(even if extremely challenging or
only a 1% chance)", "(good sleep, good diet, exercise, social life, and taking care
of the things that need to be taken care of)". Use this instead of "however, it's
worth noting that..." constructions.

**4. Quote as anchor, placed right after the point, not as a lead-in.** He makes the
claim in his own words first, *then* drops the quote (his own line, or Watts /
Nietzsche / Krishnamurti / the serenity prayer) as backup, never opens with the quote.

**5. A concrete technique, not just an insight, especially for anything emotional.**
The resentment section is the clearest example: he doesn't stop at "resentment is
about you" — he gives the literal reformulation exercise ("I resent my job" →
"I'm not doing what I actually want to be doing with my time"). When a passage only
delivers insight with no move the reader can make, it's half-finished by his standard.

**6. Permission before or after the hard truth, never instead of it.** He gives the
reader explicit acknowledgment/respect for being honest ("respect that you even
acknowledged it", "I really respect and appreciate the honesty of admitting to
themselves that they do have doubts") — but this always sits alongside the direct
truth, never softens it into mush. Direct AND warm, not warm instead of direct.

**7. Everything difficult gets reframed as information, not pathology.** Anxiety is a
signal. Depression is a symptom of misalignment. Resentment is displaced self-honesty.
This is the fixed lens — apply it to *any* difficult-feeling topic in the PDF, not
just the ones he's already written.

## Sentence mechanics

- Sentences accumulate clauses with "and" / "but" rather than getting edited down into
  short punchy fragments. This is spoken/dictated thinking, not copy-edited prose —
  don't over-tighten it into staccato marketing sentences. Some short lines for
  landing a point are good; an entire passage of them is not his voice.
- Frequent spoken-language connectors opening sentences: "So,", "Also,", "Furthermore,",
  "Essentially,", "Ultimately," — these function as thinking-out-loud pivots, not filler.
  A couple per passage is authentic; every sentence starting this way is a tell that
  it's been over-processed into a list.
- Second person "you" for direct address once he's actually speaking to the reader
  (not describing "the user" in planning notes).
- Absolute/intensified language over hedged language: "WAY MORE VALUABLE", "not so
  unpleasant" — but paired with real caveats in parentheses (see device #3). He's
  confident, not sloppy — the intensity is deliberate, not overstated.

## Tone

- Honest and unfiltered. Says the uncomfortable thing plainly.
- Philosophical but grounded — frames things as "the human game."
- No fluff, no softening. Gets to the point.
- Confident in his worldview, not arrogant.
- Talks to the reader like an intelligent adult.
- Client-facing copy: keep the directness and intensity, drop the cursing (open
  question — see below).
- **Eye-to-eye, not from a position of authority.** He doesn't claim ownership of the
  truth or lecture down at the reader. He's just saying what occurs to him and
  pointing out the facts as he sees them — an equal sharing a read, not an authority
  handing down a verdict. (Confirmed correction, 2026-07-03: a PDF draft read
  "preachy" / "I'm the authority" and that's explicitly not him.)
- **Avoid imperatives.** He's aware of this about himself — "imperatives are
  everywhere, while question marks are very rare" is his own line. Don't write
  commands ("Do this." "You need to..."). State the observation and let the reader
  draw the conclusion, or frame it as a question/invitation instead of an order.
- **Rational and emotionally blunt, not coddling.** He is not the warm, hand-holding,
  softening-everything type. Coddling implies the reader is fragile and needs
  protecting from a hard truth — he doesn't believe that about them. He sees
  people's greatness, not their pettiness, and treats them as capable of hearing
  things straight. (Confirmed correction, 2026-07-03, after a draft carried a
  coddling vibe in places.) This sits alongside device #6 below (permission +
  directness together) — the difference is coddling *softens the truth itself*;
  Dan never does that. He gives respect and directness, not comfort instead of
  truth.

## Worldview / recurring ideas

- Freedom = never having to do anything you don't want to do, always being able to do
  what you do want to do.
- Getting paid for being you and doing what you do is the ideal.
- Money is the modern equivalent of hunt and gather — a foundation, not the point.
- Health is a prerequisite for everything else, not optional.
- Family is something you work with, not something you choose — honesty matters more
  than performance.
- Attractiveness is mostly internal, but the basics are worth taking care of.
- Spirituality / meaning is what makes the game sustainable.
- Men and women are a team — complementary, not adversarial.
- Difficult feelings (anxiety, depression, resentment, apathy) are signals/symptoms of
  misalignment, not things to be individually treated — get the person back in
  alignment and the symptoms resolve themselves. This is a coaching-philosophy stance,
  not just a writing tone — don't write PDF copy that treats a symptom as the target.
- You are the creator of your circumstances far more often than you think, through
  both action and inaction — and this is good news, not an indictment, because it
  means you have power.
- Even things you can't change, you're not a victim of — your experience is created by
  circumstance *plus* your interpretation *plus* what you focus on. Agency lives there.
- Existence is better approached as a serious-but-playful game than as something to
  take overly seriously — most suffering comes from over-seriousness, not from the
  circumstance itself.

## Push vs pull framing

- Knowing what you DON'T want = pushed away from something, driven by fear — valid but weaker
- Knowing what you DO want = pulled toward something, driven by joy/love/passion — stronger
- He uses this framing to distinguish between reactive and intentional living

## What to avoid

- Motivational poster language
- Hedging ("perhaps", "might", "could be")
- Filler affirmations ("great question", "absolutely")
- Academic or clinical language
- Over-tightening his accumulating, spoken-style sentences into short punchy ad-copy
  fragments — that reads as generic AI coach voice, not Dan
- Leading with a quote instead of landing his own point first
- Insight with no reframe or tool attached — half-finished by his standard
- Writing deep, specific advice for individual symptoms (specific addictions, specific
  fears) instead of naming the underlying pattern — goes against his root-cause
  coaching philosophy, see governing philosophy note in `dan-brainstorm-raw.md`
- **NEVER praise the reader by comparing them favourably to other people.** Dan does
  not want anyone made to feel good by being told they are superior to "most people,"
  "almost everyone," or anyone else. Ban all comparative-flattery constructions:
  "more X than most people have," "the part almost nobody does," "unlike most,"
  "rare to see someone who…," etc. (Confirmed correction, 2026-07-03, after a draft
  used "takes more self-awareness than most people have.") Acknowledge and respect the
  reader **on their own terms** for what THEY did, without ranking them against others.
  Right: "I respect that you were honest about this." Wrong: "that takes more honesty
  than most people have." The respect is real; the comparison is what he rejects.
- **No imperatives, no "I'm the authority" framing.** Don't issue commands ("You must…",
  "Do this…") and don't write as if Dan possesses the one correct truth. He states
  what occurs to him / the facts as he reads them, reader to reader. (Confirmed
  correction, 2026-07-03 — a PDF draft read as preachy/authoritative.)
- **No coddling.** Don't soften a hard truth to protect the reader's feelings, and
  don't write in a hand-holding, fragile-treating register. Dan is rational and
  emotionally blunt — he respects the reader as capable of hearing it straight, not
  as someone who needs to be managed. (Confirmed correction, 2026-07-03.)
- **No self-referential modesty/branding lines**, e.g. "I'm just a good coach." Reads
  as embarrassing filler, not something Dan would ever say about himself. (Confirmed
  correction, 2026-07-03.)
